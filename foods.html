Discover Myanmar Rich Foods

The art of Myanmar’s traditional cuisine

Mohinga (မုန့်ဟင်းခါး)

Mohinga is considered Myanmar’s national dish, traditionally eaten for breakfast but loved all day long. Historical records trace its roots back to ancient times when fish was abundant along Myanmar’s riverbanks. It was a staple in the royal court and common villages alike — symbolizing unity and simplicity.

Main Ingredients

  1. Rice noodles
  2. Catfish (or any freshwater fish)
  3. Lemongrass, banana stem, ginger, and garlic
  4. Toasted rice powder (for thickening)
  5. Boiled eggs
  6. Crispy fritters and onions for toppings

In Myanmar, Mohinga represents community — it’s served during festivals, religious ceremonies, and street food stalls, reminding people of their shared traditions.

Laphet Thoke (လက်ဖက်သုတ်)

Laphet (fermented tea leaves) has been consumed since ancient Myanmar kingdoms, especially in Upper Myanmar. Tea leaf fermentation was both a preservation method and a way to celebrate victory and social bonding. Offering Laphet was a symbol of friendship and respect.

Main Ingredients

  1. Fermented tea leaves
  2. Roasted peanuts, fried garlic
  3. Toasted sesame seeds
  4. Dried shrimp
  5. Tomatoes, cabbage
  6. Lime juice and chili

Laphet Thoke is more than food — it’s a symbol of peace offerings, often used in ceremonies to settle disputes and celebrate agreements in the Konbaung dynasty.

Shan Noodles (ရှမ်းခေါက်ဆွဲ)

Shan noodles originate from Shan State, where the Shan ethnic group resides. The Shan kingdom had trade connections with China and Thailand, influencing the noodle’s mild, clean, and savory flavor. It’s a classic example of ethnic diversity in Myanmar food culture.

Main Ingredients

  1. Rice noodles (flat or round)
  2. Marinated chicken or pork (with light soy sauce)
  3. Fried garlic oil
  4. Pickled mustard greens
  5. Tomato-based sauce
  6. Fresh spring onions

Shan noodles are associated with hospitality in the hills and are beloved by both locals and travelers. They highlight the simplicity and warmth of Shan culture, often served in Shan tea shops and homes.
